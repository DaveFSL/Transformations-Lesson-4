Key Vocabulary
Combined Transformation

Two or more transformations applied one after the other to the same shape.

Transformation Sequence

The ordered list of transformations: Step 1, Step 2, Step 3…

Tracking Table

A table recording coordinates after each step: Original → After Step 1 → After Step 2.

Intermediate Position

Where the shape is after step 1 but before step 2 — the in-between state.

Invariant

A property that stays the same through all transformations — e.g. size and shape (congruence).

Coordinate Rule

A rule describing a transformation using notation: (x,y) → (x+3, y−2).

Step-by-Step: Translate then Reflect

We apply two transformations in order to Triangle A(1,1) B(3,1) C(2,3). Click through each step below and follow along on your grid paper. Use a different colour at each step.

Ready. Set up your grid. Draw your Cartesian plane with both axes labelled. Mark the origin and number the axes. We will plot Triangle A at A(1,1) B(3,1) C(2,3) first, then apply two transformations in sequence.
Shape A plotted. Plot A(1,1), B(3,1) and C(2,3) and connect them. These are your starting coordinates. Record them in column 1 of your tracking table: Original.
Step 1: Translate 3 right. Every vertex moves 3 squares right — x increases by 3, y stays the same. A(1,1) → A'(4,1). B(3,1) → B'(6,1). C(2,3) → C'(5,3). Draw the new triangle in a different colour. Record in column 2: After Step 1.
Step 2: Reflect A' over the y-axis. Every x-value changes sign — y stays the same. A'(4,1) → A''(-4,1). B'(6,1) → B''(-6,1). C'(5,3) → C''(-5,3). Draw in a third colour. The mirror line is the y-axis (orange dashed line). Record in column 3: After Step 2.
The full journey: A → A' → A''. Shape A (faded blue) was translated to A' (solid blue), then reflected to A'' (orange). Your tracking table should have three complete columns. The final shape is congruent to the original — same size and shape, different position and orientation.

Introducing Rotation on the Cartesian Plane

Rotation uses a centre of rotation (today: the origin) and an angle and direction. Click through the two-step demo below.

Shape P before rotation. Triangle P(1,2) Q(3,2) R(2,4) plotted in Quadrant I. The purple dot at the origin (0,0) is our centre of rotation. Use tracing paper: trace this triangle, then hold your pencil at (0,0) and turn the paper 90° clockwise.
After 90° clockwise rotation. The arc shows the direction of turn. P(1,2) → P'(2,−1). Q(3,2) → Q'(2,−3). R(2,4) → R'(4,−2). Notice: each new coordinate is (y, −x) from the original. The shape has moved into Quadrant IV. It is still congruent — same size and shape.

Original order (translate then reflect): A(1,2) → (3,1) → (-3,1).

★1 — Rotation coordinate rule

The diagram shows a square rotated 90° clockwise (then 180°, 270°) around the origin. Use the coordinates shown to find the pattern: what is the coordinate rule for a 90° clockwise rotation? A 180° rotation? A 270° clockwise rotation?

Pick one vertex from the 0° position. Write its coordinates. Find the same vertex after 90° CW. Write those coordinates. What changed? What is the relationship between the original (x,y) and the new (x,y)?
Try the point (3,1). After 90° CW it goes to (1,−3). What happened to x? What happened to y? Check your rule with another vertex.
For 180°: (3,1) goes to (−3,−1). For 270° CW: (3,1) goes to (−1,3). Can you write each as a rule involving x and y?
Rotation rules (around the origin):
90° CW: (x,y) → (y, −x)
180°: (x,y) → (−x, −y)
270° CW (= 90° anticlockwise): (x,y) → (−y, x)
360°: back to (x,y) — full circle!

Check with (3,1): 90° CW → (1,−3) ✔ 180° → (−3,−1) ✔ 270° CW → (−1,3) ✔
★2 — Generalise: when does order matter?

You have now tested order for translation + reflection. Can you find a pair of transformations where order never matters? Investigate with at least two different starting shapes. Write a general claim and justify it with coordinate evidence.

Try two translations: e.g. translate 3 right then translate 2 up. Now reverse the order. What do you get each time? Is the result always the same regardless of order?
Answer: Two translations always commute — order never matters. Translating (a,b) then (c,d) gives (x+a+c, y+b+d). Reversing gives (x+c+a, y+d+b) — the same result, because addition is commutative. Most other pairs (translation + reflection, rotation + reflection) do NOT commute. Two rotations around the same centre also commute. This connects to a deep idea in mathematics: some operations are commutative, some are not.
